Variance Swap Replication: From Itô’s Lemma to a Tradeable Portfolio

方差互换复制：从伊藤引理到可交易组合

✎ Dong Liu 📅 July 2026 🔗 LinkedIn 💬 Comments welcome

Abstract

A variance swap pays the difference between realised variance and a fixed strike agreed at inception. Its appeal lies in providing pure, model-free exposure to volatility — yet the claim of “model-free” replication is non-trivial and rests on a precise mathematical identity. This note derives that identity from first principles, starting from Itô’s lemma applied to $\ln S_t$, and shows how the resulting two-legged replication strategy — a continuously delta-hedged stock position and a static strip of vanilla options — maps to instruments that are actually tradeable.

The static leg is constructed via the Carr–Madan spanning formula, which decomposes any smooth payoff $f(S_T)$ into cash, a forward, and a continuum of options weighted by the second derivative $f''(K)$. For the log contract, this weight is $1/K^2$, giving the familiar VIX-style integral over out-of-the-money puts and calls. A Monte Carlo backtest under both GBM and Heston dynamics confirms that the replication portfolio tracks realised variance with tracking error an order of magnitude smaller than the variance itself, and that error shrinks as rebalancing frequency increases — consistent with the theoretical $O(\Delta t)$ discretisation residual.

The practical limits of replication — finite strike grids, truncated moneyness ranges, discrete rebalancing, and jump risk — are quantified explicitly. These limits are precisely the terms on which real QIS products are priced, and understanding them separates informed product analysis from marketing-deck consumption.

Keywords: variance swap, log contract, Itô’s lemma, Carr–Madan spanning, static replication, realised variance, VIX, Heston model, QIS

I. The Replication Identity: Itô’s Lemma Applied to ln S

1.1 Setup and Goal

Let $S_t$ be the price of a non-dividend-paying asset satisfying the stochastic differential equation

$$dS_t = \mu_t S_t\,dt + \sigma_t S_t\,dW_t,\tag{1}$$

where $\mu_t$ and $\sigma_t$ are adapted processes and $W_t$ is a standard Brownian motion. Dividing through by $S_t$ gives the return process:

$$\frac{dS_t}{S_t} = \mu_t\,dt + \sigma_t\,dW_t.\tag{2}$$

A variance swap pays, at maturity $T$, the quantity $\int_0^T \sigma_t^2\,dt$ (realised variance) minus a fixed strike $K_{\text{var}}$ determined at inception. The central challenge is that $\sigma_t$ is not directly tradeable: we must express $\int_0^T \sigma_t^2\,dt$ purely as a portfolio payoff in stocks and options.

1.2 Itô’s Lemma and the Key Cancellation

In ordinary calculus the chain rule gives $df(S) = f'(S)\,dS$. For a stochastic process, the correct analogue includes a second-order correction arising because $(dW_t)^2 = dt$ — a fact with no deterministic parallel:

Itô’s Lemma (scalar diffusion)

Let $f \in C^2$. Then $$df(S_t) = f'(S_t)\,dS_t + \tfrac{1}{2}f''(S_t)\,\sigma_t^2 S_t^2\,dt.$$ The correction term $\tfrac{1}{2}f''\sigma^2 S^2\,dt$ is of order $dt$ because $(dW_t)^2 = dt$, not zero. It vanishes in deterministic calculus but is essential here.

Apply this to $f(S) = \ln S$, with $f'(S) = 1/S$ and $f''(S) = -1/S^2$:

$$d\ln S_t = \frac{dS_t}{S_t} + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(-\frac{1}{S_t^2}\right)\sigma_t^2 S_t^2\,dt = \frac{dS_t}{S_t} - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_t^2\,dt.\tag{3}$$

Equations (2) and (3) share exactly the same stochastic term $\sigma_t\,dW_t$. Subtracting (3) from (2) eliminates it entirely:

$$\frac{dS_t}{S_t} - d\ln S_t = \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_t^2\,dt.\tag{4}$$

Key insight: The subtraction in (4) is a purification step. Both $dS_t/S_t$ and $d\ln S_t$ contain the unhedgeable stochastic term $\sigma_t\,dW_t$; subtracting removes it, isolating the quadratic variation — precisely the variance we wish to replicate.

Integrating (4) from $0$ to $T$ and multiplying by 2 yields the fundamental identity:

Proposition 1 — Variance Swap Replication Identity

For any continuous semimartingale $S_t$ satisfying (1),

$$\int_0^T \sigma_t^2\,dt \;=\; 2\!\left[\,\underbrace{\int_0^T \frac{dS_t}{S_t}}_{\scriptstyle\text{dynamic leg}} \;-\; \underbrace{\ln\frac{S_T}{S_0}}_{\scriptstyle\text{log-contract leg}}\,\right].\tag{5}$$

This identity holds pathwise — for every realised trajectory, not merely in expectation — and is independent of any distributional assumption on $\sigma_t$.

1.3 Interpretation of the Two Legs

  • Dynamic leg $\int_0^T dS_t/S_t$: the cumulative P&L from continuously rebalancing to hold exactly $1/S_t$ units of stock at each instant, maintaining a constant one-unit notional. This requires ongoing trading but involves only the underlying asset.
  • Log-contract leg $-\ln(S_T/S_0)$: a payoff that depends only on the terminal-to-initial price ratio. Because it is a function solely of $S_T$, it can be replicated by a static portfolio of vanilla options assembled at $t=0$ and held without adjustment to maturity.

II. The Static Leg: Carr–Madan Log-Contract Spanning

2.1 Any Smooth Payoff as a Strip of Options

The Carr–Madan spanning formula states that any twice-differentiable payoff $f(S_T)$ can be decomposed, for an arbitrary anchor $F$, as:

$$f(S_T) = f(F) + f'(F)(S_T - F) + \int_0^F f''(K)(K-S_T)^+\,dK + \int_F^\infty f''(K)(S_T-K)^+\,dK.\tag{6}$$

The four terms correspond to: a cash position $f(F)$, a forward with slope $f'(F)$, a strip of out-of-the-money puts weighted by $f''(K)$ for $K < F$, and a strip of out-of-the-money calls weighted by $f''(K)$ for $K \geq F$. Options with strike $K$ act as localised slope-correction tools: each infinitesimal option contributes a kink of size $f''(K)\,dK$ to the payoff profile at the point $K$.

2.2 Applying the Formula to $-\ln(S_T/F)$

Set $f(S_T) = -\ln(S_T/F)$. Then $f'(S) = -1/S$ and $f''(S) = 1/S^2$. Evaluating at the anchor: $f(F) = 0$ and $f'(F) = -1/F$. Substituting into (6):

$$-\ln\frac{S_T}{F} = -\frac{S_T - F}{F} + \int_0^F \frac{(K-S_T)^+}{K^2}\,dK + \int_F^\infty \frac{(S_T-K)^+}{K^2}\,dK.\tag{7}$$

To recover $-\ln(S_T/S_0)$ as required by identity (5), note that

$$-\ln\frac{S_T}{S_0} = -\ln\frac{S_T}{F} - \ln\frac{F}{S_0},$$

so the full log-contract payoff is obtained by adding the deterministic constant $-\ln(F/S_0) = -rT$ (under no dividends). This cash adjustment is incorporated at inception.

Proposition 2 — Static Replication of the Log Contract

The payoff $-\ln(S_T/S_0)$ is replicated exactly by a static portfolio assembled at $t=0$ and held to maturity without adjustment:

$$-\ln\frac{S_T}{S_0} = \underbrace{-\frac{S_T-F}{F}}_{\scriptstyle\text{short forward}} + \underbrace{\int_0^F \frac{(K-S_T)^+}{K^2}\,dK}_{\scriptstyle\text{OTM puts,}\ 1/K^2} + \underbrace{\int_F^\infty \frac{(S_T-K)^+}{K^2}\,dK}_{\scriptstyle\text{OTM calls,}\ 1/K^2} - \ln\frac{F}{S_0}.\tag{8}$$

The weight $1/K^2$ assigns proportionally more weight to lower-strike options, reflecting the convexity of the log function. This is precisely the weighting scheme used by CBOE in the VIX methodology.

2.3 The Fair Variance Strike

Taking the risk-neutral expectation of both sides of (5) and substituting (8), the fair variance strike — the level at which the swap has zero inception value — is:

$$K_{\text{var}} = \frac{2e^{rT}}{T}\left[\int_0^F \frac{P(K)}{K^2}\,dK + \int_F^\infty \frac{C(K)}{K^2}\,dK\right],\tag{9}$$

where $P(K)$ and $C(K)$ are market prices of out-of-the-money puts and calls. This is the CBOE VIX formula (up to the square root).

Corollary 1 — Model-Free Nature of the Fair Strike

Under continuous-path dynamics, $K_{\text{var}}$ in (9) equals $\mathbb{E}^Q\!\left[\int_0^T\sigma_t^2\,dt\right]$ for any stochastic volatility model consistent with observed option prices. The replication in (5) and (8) achieves this payoff without specifying a model for $\sigma_t$.

III. Monte Carlo Verification

3.1 Implementation

We simulate $10{,}000$ paths under two dynamics and measure the tracking error between realised variance and the replication portfolio P&L. The dynamic leg uses daily discrete rebalancing ($N = 252$ steps); the static leg uses 200 strikes uniformly spaced over $[30\%, 250\%]$ of $S_0$ with trapezoidal integration; realised variance is $\sum_i (\ln S_{t_{i+1}}/S_{t_i})^2 / T$.

import numpy as np

def replicate_variance_swap(S, T, F, S0, strikes):
    # S: price path array, shape (n_paths, N+1)
    # T: maturity in years
    # F: forward price S0 * exp(r*T)
    N = S.shape[1] - 1

    # Realised variance: sum of squared log-returns / T
    log_rets = np.diff(np.log(S), axis=1)
    RV = np.sum(log_rets ** 2, axis=1) / T

    # Dynamic leg: discrete sum of dS/S
    dyn = np.sum(np.diff(S, axis=1) / S[:, :-1], axis=1)

    # Static leg: Carr-Madan option strip + forward + cash correction
    S_T   = S[:, -1]
    dK    = strikes[1] - strikes[0]
    K     = strikes[None, :]        # broadcast over paths
    S_col = S_T[:, None]
    put_pay  = np.where(K < F, np.maximum(K - S_col, 0), 0.0)
    call_pay = np.where(K >= F, np.maximum(S_col - K, 0), 0.0)
    option_leg  = np.sum((put_pay + call_pay) / K**2 * dK, axis=1)
    forward_leg = -(S_T - F) / F
    cash_adj    = -np.log(F / S0)   # bridges -ln(S_T/F) to -ln(S_T/S0)
    static_leg  = forward_leg + option_leg + cash_adj

    # Replicated P&L and tracking error
    replicated     = 2.0 * (dyn + static_leg) / T
    tracking_error = RV - replicated
    return RV, replicated, tracking_error

IV. Practical Limits and Real-World Gaps

The theoretical identity (5) is exact; its practical implementation involves four gaps that generate tracking error and form the basis of pricing adjustments in real QIS products.

4.1 Finite Strike Grid and Truncated Moneyness Range

The integrals in (8) extend from zero to infinity, but traded strikes occupy a finite interval $[K_{\min}, K_{\max}]$. The truncation error is asymmetric: negligible within the grid, but growing sharply when $S_T$ exits the covered range. For a grid spanning $[50\%, 200\%]$ of $S_0$, a terminal price of $S_T = 220$ (a 120% gain) incurs a replication error of order 0.12 — comparable in magnitude to the log-contract payoff itself. This tail truncation risk is what a variance swap dealer retains and prices into the bid-offer spread.

Discretisation within the covered range

Approximating the continuous integral by a sum over $n$ equally spaced strikes introduces an $O(1/n^2)$ error (trapezoidal rule on a smooth integrand). With 80–100 traded strikes, this is typically below $10^{-4}$ in variance units — negligible relative to bid-offer.

4.2 Discrete Rebalancing of the Dynamic Leg

Daily rebalancing introduces an $O(\Delta t)$ discretisation error per period. The aggregate error has standard deviation of order $\sigma^2\sqrt{T}$, small relative to the variance swap notional but non-zero. In stressed markets, bid-offer on the delta-hedge compounds this.

4.3 Jump Risk

Identity (5) assumes $S_t$ has continuous paths. Under a jump-diffusion process with jump size $J$, the quadratic variation receives a contribution $J^2$ from each jump, but the log-contract payoff captures only $\ln(1+J) \approx J - J^2/2 + \cdots$. The gap, approximately $J^2/2$ per jump, means the replication under-captures variance when large jumps occur. This is the “jump convexity” premium: in practice, variance swap strikes trade at a premium to expected realised variance partly to compensate the dealer for absorbing unhedgeable jump risk.

4.4 Funding, Dividends, and Transaction Costs

The dynamic leg requires continuous financing of the stock position at the repo rate. Discrete dividend payments shift the replication P&L and must be adjusted for in (9). Transaction costs on daily rebalancing are small per step but cumulative, with a path-dependent drag that depends on the volatility of $\sigma_t$.

Implication for QIS product analysis: A variance swap supplement or marketing deck that claims “model-free” replication is technically correct only within the range of traded strikes, under continuous rebalancing, and in the absence of jumps. The deviation from these idealised conditions — strike grid truncation, rebalancing discretisation, jump premium, and funding adjustment — is precisely where the structured product desk earns its margin. Reading the term sheet for the moneyness range of the replicating strip, the rebalancing convention, and any cap on realised variance reveals the structure of that margin.

A.2 — Path Simulation (GBM and Heston)

GBM uses the exact log-Euler discretisation, which has no bias regardless of step size. Heston uses the full-truncation Euler scheme: whenever the variance process v turns negative due to discretisation, it is clamped to zero before taking the square root. Always check the Feller condition 2 κ θ ≥ ξ²; when it is violated, the variance process hits zero frequently and the Euler scheme becomes unreliable. 

def simulate_gbm(S0, r, sigma, T, N, n_paths=1, seed=None):
    """
    Simulate GBM paths using the exact log-Euler scheme.

    Parameters
    ----------
    S0      : initial price
    r       : risk-free rate (continuously compounded)
    sigma   : constant volatility
    T       : maturity in years
    N       : number of time steps
    n_paths : number of Monte Carlo paths
    seed    : random seed for reproducibility

    Returns
    -------
    S : ndarray of shape (n_paths, N+1).  S[:, 0] = S0 for all paths.
    """
    rng     = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    dt      = T / N
    Z       = rng.standard_normal((n_paths, N))
    log_inc = (r - 0.5 * sigma**2) * dt + sigma * np.sqrt(dt) * Z
    S       = np.exp(np.log(S0) + np.cumsum(log_inc, axis=1))
    return np.hstack([np.full((n_paths, 1), S0), S])


def simulate_heston(S0, r, v0, kappa, theta, xi, rho, T, N, n_paths=1, seed=None):
    """
    Simulate Heston stochastic-volatility paths (full-truncation Euler scheme).

    Model:
        dS_t / S_t = r dt + sqrt(v_t) dW1_t
        dv_t       = kappa*(theta - v_t) dt + xi*sqrt(v_t) dW2_t
        corr(dW1, dW2) = rho

    Feller condition for v_t to stay non-negative: 2*kappa*theta >= xi**2.
    When violated, the variance process hits zero frequently; the full-truncation
    scheme clamps v to 0 before taking square roots, keeping the simulation
    stable at the cost of some discretisation bias in extreme paths.

    Returns
    -------
    S : ndarray (n_paths, N+1)   price paths
    v : ndarray (n_paths, N+1)   variance paths
    """
    rng = np.random.default_rng(seed)
    dt  = T / N
    S   = np.zeros((n_paths, N + 1));  S[:, 0] = S0
    v   = np.zeros((n_paths, N + 1));  v[:, 0] = v0

    for t in range(N):
        Z1 = rng.standard_normal(n_paths)
        Z2 = rng.standard_normal(n_paths)
        W1 = Z1
        W2 = rho * Z1 + np.sqrt(1 - rho**2) * Z2   # correlated Brownian

        v_pos = np.maximum(v[:, t], 0.0)             # full truncation
        sqv   = np.sqrt(v_pos)

        S[:, t+1] = S[:, t] * np.exp(
            (r - 0.5 * v_pos) * dt + sqv * np.sqrt(dt) * W1)
        v[:, t+1] = (v[:, t]
                     + kappa * (theta - v_pos) * dt
                     + xi * sqv * np.sqrt(dt) * W2)
    return S, v

A.3 — Realised Variance

Industry-standard definition: sum of squared log-returns divided by T. Each log-return has variance σ² dt; summing N of them gives σ²T, and dividing by T recovers the annualised σ² scale. A common mistake is to multiply by N/T instead of dividing by T, which produces a result that grows with N. 

def realised_variance(S, T):
    """
    Annualised realised variance from a matrix of price paths.

    Each log-return r_i = ln(S_{i+1}/S_i) has variance sigma^2 * dt.
    Summing N of them gives sigma^2 * T; dividing by T recovers sigma^2.

    Parameters
    ----------
    S : ndarray (n_paths, N+1)
    T : float, maturity in years

    Returns
    -------
    RV : ndarray (n_paths,)
    """
    log_rets = np.diff(np.log(S), axis=1)   # shape (n_paths, N)
    return np.sum(log_rets**2, axis=1) / T

A.4 — The Two Replication Legs

Dynamic leg: hold 1/St units of stock at each step so the notional stays constant; cumulative P&L approximates ∫ dSt/St. Static leg: Carr–Madan with anchor F spans −ln(ST/F); adding the cash adjustment −ln(F/S0) = −rT shifts this to −ln(ST/S0) as required by identity (5). Omitting that cash term is the single most common implementation bug: it causes the replicated P&L to equal systematically twice the true realised variance. 

def dynamic_leg(S):
    """
    Discrete approximation of the dynamic hedge integral int_0^T dS_t / S_t.

    At each time step, hold 1/S_t units of stock so that the notional stays
    constant at one unit of currency.  P&L per step = dS_t / S_t.
    Cumulative P&L over [0,T] approximates the stochastic integral.

    Parameters
    ----------
    S : ndarray (n_paths, N+1)

    Returns
    -------
    dyn : ndarray (n_paths,)
    """
    return np.sum(np.diff(S, axis=1) / S[:, :-1], axis=1)


def static_leg(S0, F, S_T, strikes):
    """
    Carr-Madan static replication of -ln(S_T / S0).

    The spanning formula with anchor F (equation 8 of the article) gives
    -ln(S_T / F):

        -ln(S_T/F) = -(S_T - F)/F
                     + int_0^F   (1/K^2)(K  - S_T)^+  dK   [OTM puts ]
                     + int_F^inf (1/K^2)(S_T - K )^+  dK   [OTM calls]

    To shift from -ln(S_T/F) to -ln(S_T/S0) as required by identity (5),
    add the deterministic cash adjustment -ln(F/S0) = -rT (no dividends).
    Omitting this term is a common bug: it causes the replicated P&L to
    systematically equal twice the true realised variance.

    Parameters
    ----------
    S0      : initial price
    F       : forward price  S0 * exp(r*T)
    S_T     : terminal prices, shape (n_paths,)
    strikes : discrete strike grid, shape (n_strikes,)

    Returns
    -------
    payoff : ndarray (n_paths,)
    """
    dK  = np.diff(strikes);  dK = np.append(dK, dK[-1])  # trapezoidal weights
    K   = strikes[None, :];  dK = dK[None, :]             # broadcast over paths
    Sc  = S_T[:, None]

    put_pnl  = np.where(K <  F, np.maximum(K  - Sc, 0.0), 0.0)
    call_pnl = np.where(K >= F, np.maximum(Sc - K,  0.0), 0.0)

    option_leg  = np.sum((put_pnl + call_pnl) / K**2 * dK, axis=1)
    forward_leg = -(S_T - F) / F
    cash_adj    = -np.log(F / S0)   # bridges -ln(S_T/F) to -ln(S_T/S0)

    return forward_leg + option_leg + cash_adj


def replicate_variance_swap(S, T, F, S0, strikes):
    """
    Compute realised variance, replicated P&L, and tracking error.

    Core identity (equation 5 of the article, exact in continuous time):
        int_0^T sigma_t^2 dt = (2/T) * [dynamic_leg(S) + static_leg(...)]

    Parameters
    ----------
    S       : ndarray (n_paths, N+1)
    T       : maturity in years
    F       : forward price
    S0      : initial price
    strikes : discrete strike grid

    Returns
    -------
    RV             : ndarray (n_paths,)   annualised realised variance
    replicated     : ndarray (n_paths,)   replication portfolio P&L
    tracking_error : ndarray (n_paths,)   RV - replicated
    """
    dyn        = dynamic_leg(S)
    stat       = static_leg(S0, F, S[:, -1], strikes)
    RV         = realised_variance(S, T)
    replicated = 2.0 * (dyn + stat) / T
    return RV, replicated, RV - replicated

A.5 — Fair Variance Strike (CBOE VIX Formula)

Equation (9): price OTM options across the strike grid and integrate with weight 1/K². Black-Scholes is used here purely for illustration; in production, substitute market mid-prices. The formula remains model-free as long as the underlying has continuous paths. 

def bs_price(S0, K, r, sigma, T, flag):
    """Black-Scholes European option price.  flag: 'call' or 'put'."""
    if T <= 0:
        return max(S0 - K, 0.0) if flag == 'call' else max(K - S0, 0.0)
    d1 = (np.log(S0 / K) + (r + 0.5 * sigma**2) * T) / (sigma * np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma * np.sqrt(T)
    if flag == 'call':
        return S0 * norm.cdf(d1) - K * np.exp(-r * T) * norm.cdf(d2)
    return K * np.exp(-r * T) * norm.cdf(-d2) - S0 * norm.cdf(-d1)


def fair_variance_strike(S0, F, r, T, sigma_iv, strikes):
    """
    Model-free fair variance strike via equation (9) of the article:

        K_var = (2 * e^{rT} / T) * sum_K  OTM_price(K) / K^2 * dK

    OTM prices are computed here with Black-Scholes under a flat implied
    volatility sigma_iv.  In production, substitute market mid-prices;
    the formula remains valid regardless of the smile shape.

    Parameters
    ----------
    sigma_iv : implied volatility used for Black-Scholes pricing

    Returns
    -------
    K_var : float   (annualised variance units, same scale as sigma^2)
    """
    dK    = np.diff(strikes);  dK = np.append(dK, dK[-1])
    total = sum(
        bs_price(S0, K, r, sigma_iv, T, 'put' if K < F else 'call') / K**2 * dk
        for K, dk in zip(strikes, dK)
    )
    return (2 * np.exp(r * T) / T) * total

S0      = 100.0    # initial stock price
r       = 0.02     # continuously compounded risk-free rate

# ── Heston backtest ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
v0, kappa, theta, xi, rho = 0.04, 3.0, 0.04, 0.35, -0.7
feller = 2 * kappa * theta - xi**2
print(f"Feller condition 2*kappa*theta - xi^2 = {feller:.4f}  "
      f"({'OK: variance process stays non-negative' if feller >= 0 else 'VIOLATED: discretisation may be unstable'})")

S_h, _            = simulate_heston(S0, r, v0, kappa, theta, xi, rho, T, N, n_paths, seed)

# ── Figure 3: discretisation convergence (log-log) ───────────────────────────
Ns   = [12, 52, 126, 252, 504, 1000]
stds = []
rng2 = np.random.default_rng(7)
for N_ in Ns:
    dt_ = T / N_
    Z_  = rng2.standard_normal((5000, N_))
    li_ = (r - 0.5 * sigma**2) * dt_ + sigma * np.sqrt(dt_) * Z_
    S_  = np.hstack([np.full((5000, 1), S0),
                     np.exp(np.log(S0) + np.cumsum(li_, axis=1))])
    rv_  = realised_variance(S_, T)
    dyn_ = dynamic_leg(S_)
    # Use the exact log-contract (no static-replication error) to isolate
    # the dynamic-leg discretisation error alone.
    lc_  = -np.log(S_[:, -1] / S0)
    stds.append((rv_ - 2 * (dyn_ + lc_) / T).std())

ax = axes[2]
ax.set_facecolor('#fafafa')
